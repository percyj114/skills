## 1. The "Unpopular Opinion" Format

### What it looks like
```

[1-2 sentences defending it]

[The real insight people are missing]

[Close with conviction â€” not a question]
```

### Why it works
Triggers immediate response (agree or disagree, both = engagement). People feel compelled to reply. The word "unpopular" primes the reader to pay attention because they expect something different â€” and if you deliver, they share.

### Example
```

Most accounts that "post consistently" just produce noise. The algo rewards engagement rate, not output rate.

Three high-performing posts beat 21 mediocre ones every single time.

Post less. Make each one count.
```

### When to use it
- When you genuinely believe something the mainstream doesn't
- When you want to spark debate in your niche
- On X/Twitter and LinkedIn (performs weaker on Instagram unless it's a Reel hook)
- NOT when your "unpopular opinion" is actually mainstream. That reads fake.

## 2. The "I Spent X Doing Y, Here's What I Learned" Format

### What it looks like
```
I spent [time/money/effort] [doing specific thing].

Here's what I learned: [key insight]

[2-5 numbered lessons or observations]

[Honest close â€” what you'd do differently or the one takeaway]
```

### Why it works
Combines credibility (you did the thing) with value (here's the shortcut). People pay attention to lessons from experience because it saves them time and effort. The specificity of "I spent X" signals real data, not opinion.

### Example
```
I spent 6 months posting daily on LinkedIn.

Here's what I actually learned:

1. Long posts with line breaks outperformed short ones 4x
2. The first line determined everything. Not the topic. The first line.
3. Commenting on others' posts before mine posted doubled my reach
4. My worst content ideas got the most traction

The one thing I'd change: I'd have quit worrying about going viral and focused on being consistent. Consistency compounded. Virality didn't.
```

### When to use it
- When you have actual experience to draw from (don't fabricate)
- Long-form LinkedIn posts, newsletter sections, Twitter threads
- Great for authority building in a niche

## 5. The Contrarian Take Format

### What it looks like
```
[Common advice everyone gives]: [dismiss it]

Here's the truth: [your counter-position]

[Evidence or reasoning]

[The nuanced close â€” acknowledge the exceptions or double down]
```

### Why it works
Goes against the grain without being contrarian for its own sake. The best contrarian takes offer a genuine alternative view, not just negation. People share these because they've thought the same thing but never said it, or they vehemently disagree and want others to weigh in.

### Example
```

Here's the truth: consistency is a habit, not a volume metric.

Posting every day trains you to produce mediocre content because you're optimizing for output, not quality. You burn out by day 18, quit by day 23, and learn nothing useful.

What actually works: identify 2 formats you're good at. Post those formats 2â€“3x per week. Do it for 6 months.

Volume doesn't build an audience. Good content repeated consistently does.
```

### When to use it
- When you can back up your position
- X/Twitter, LinkedIn (high comment-bait potential)
- Not for every post â€” contrarian voices that are always contrarian become noise

| Goal | Best Format |
|------|------------|
| Spark debate | Unpopular Opinion or Contrarian Take |

## Stacking Formats
The highest-performing posts often combine two formats:
- **Contrarian Take + Simple Framework** = "Everyone says X. Here's why Y is actually right: [numbered list]"
- **Nobody Talks About + I Spent X** = "Nobody talks about this, but after 6 months of testing..."
- **Unpopular Opinion + Simple Framework** = "Unpopular opinion: [take]. Here's the 3-step proof: [list]"
